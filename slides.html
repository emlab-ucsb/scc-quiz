Why put a price on carbon?

Climate policy is a cost–benefit problem:

  • Cutting emissions costs something today.
  • It avoids damages tomorrow — heat, floods, lost crops, lives.

To weigh them we need one number: the price of a tonne of CO₂.

This number quietly underlies trillions in regulatory decisions. Today we poke at how shaky — and how high — it might really be.

The Social Cost of Carbon

The present value of the damages from emitting one extra tonne of CO₂.

It’s the workhorse behind federal cost–benefit analysis. A higher SCC justifies more aggressive climate policy.

Rennert et al. (2022): a higher number

In Nature — “Comprehensive evidence implies a higher social cost of CO₂”:

  • Central estimate: $185 per tonne (2% discount rate).
  • 3.6× the $51 the U.S. government was using.
  • Range roughly $44 – $413 (5th–95th percentile).

Weitzman’s Dismal Theorem

With fat-tailed catastrophic risk, the “correct” carbon price could be…

  • A. A modest, finite number
  • B. Effectively unbounded
  • C. Zero — too uncertain to act
  • D. Whatever the discount rate implies

This is the provocation. Let the split show before explaining.

Fat tails break the tidy number

Weitzman (2009): if catastrophe sits in a fat tail and marginal utility blows up as consumption collapses, expected damages can be unbounded — standard cost–benefit “breaks down.”

  • A single $185 hides enormous tail risk.
  • The argument: structural uncertainty can matter more than the discount rate.
